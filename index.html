How I Work

How I approach
problems

01 — Behaviour First

Start from behaviour, not assumptions

I don't start with what I think users want. I start with what they actually do — where they drop off, where they call support, where the agent submits incorrect data. The behaviour is the signal. The assumption is a hypothesis to be tested.

02 — Systems Thinking

Design systems, not one-off fixes

One failed loan application is a support ticket. A thousand failed applications is a product problem. I look for the structural pattern — whether it's an affordability threshold, a communication gap, or a broken handoff — and design the system that fixes the root cause.

03 — Field → Product

Bridge field insights with product decisions

Some of my best product decisions came from a conversation in a Ruiru market stall or a Nairobi repair shop. Real environments reveal what dashboards hide. I build the habit of going to where the friction lives — then translating it into sprint-ready user stories.

Copia Kenya · Agent Credit & Loan System

Building a Credit & Collections
System from the Ground Up

How I translated credit policy into a scalable agent loan product — mapping eligibility rules, building automated collections infrastructure, and designing the agent-facing tools that made credit accessible and manageable across hundreds of field agents in rural Kenya.

Company
Copia Kenya Ltd
My Role
Business Analyst / PM
Domain
Credit · Fintech · Agent Networks
Market
Kenya · B2B2C
Context

A distributed agent network with no formal credit infrastructure

Copia Kenya operated through a network of hundreds of agents — micro-entrepreneurs who took orders from rural customers and fulfilled them through Copia's supply chain. These agents needed access to credit to stock inventory and grow their businesses, but there was no formal loan product, no eligibility system, and no collections mechanism in place.

The credit function existed as a manual process scattered across spreadsheets, credit team judgement calls, and informal tracking. The product gap was significant — and the risk of getting it wrong was real.

Problem

Three problems compounding each other simultaneously

  • No structured eligibility: Credit decisions were inconsistent and dependent on individual credit officers — there was no systematic way to assess agent risk at scale
  • No automated collections: Loan repayments were tracked manually, creating delays, errors, and a growing default risk that was hard to see in real time
  • Agent-facing tools were absent: Agents had no visibility into their loan status, repayment schedules, or eligibility — leading to confusion, disputes, and low trust in the product
What I Did

Translated policy into a working product system

  • Mapped the entire credit journey — from agent application to approval, disbursement, repayment, and default — identifying every decision point that needed a rule, a tool, or a workflow
  • Designed the loan eligibility framework: Defined scoring criteria based on order history, tenure, repayment behaviour, and agent tier — then translated these into the Odoo loan module configuration
  • Built automated collections enforcement: Designed the logic for automatic loan deductions from agent commissions and order payments, reducing the need for manual follow-up and eliminating the spreadsheet-based tracking that preceded it
  • Managed the Odoo loan module end-to-end: Configuration, UAT, agent training documentation, and ongoing iteration based on credit team and agent feedback
  • Designed agent-facing visibility tools: Loan status, repayment schedule tracking, and eligibility notifications — so agents could understand their credit standing without calling the credit team
Key Decisions

Where I had to make real calls

  • Automate collections first, not last: I pushed to prioritise the collections automation before scaling disbursements — because growing the loan book without a reliable repayment system would compound the default risk exponentially
  • Build for the agent, not the credit officer: The natural instinct was to build tools for credit analysts. I argued for investing in agent-facing features first — eligibility visibility, repayment tracking, status notifications — because agent trust was the product's biggest adoption risk
  • Standardise eligibility criteria before scaling: Rather than letting individual credit officers apply their own judgement, I pushed for a documented, rule-based eligibility framework. Consistency was the only way to scale fairly and reduce the risk of perceived discrimination in the field
  • Microinsurance integration as a risk tool, not just a benefit: Proposed embedding insurance premium collection into the loan repayment flow — framing it not as a feature add-on, but as a structural way to reduce default exposure from income shocks
Challenges

What made this hard

  • Working across credit, operations, engineering, and field teams with different risk appetites, timelines, and definitions of "done"
  • Odoo's limitations required significant configuration work to map Copia's non-standard loan structures — some edge cases needed workarounds that had to be documented carefully
  • Agent trust was fragile — any collections error that hit an agent incorrectly could damage the entire programme's credibility with the field force
  • Rural infrastructure: Agents in low-connectivity areas needed processes that could function offline and sync later — standard fintech assumptions didn't hold
Broader Outcomes

What the system enabled

  • A repeatable, scalable loan product that moved from manual chaos to a managed system — with defined eligibility rules, automated collections, and agent-facing visibility replacing spreadsheets and informal tracking
  • Credit decisions became consistent and auditable — the eligibility framework eliminated officer-by-officer variation and created a defensible, documented standard for approvals and rejections
  • Collections automation reduced the credit team's manual workload significantly and created a real-time view of repayment performance that hadn't existed before
  • The microinsurance integration created a new recurring revenue stream while simultaneously reducing default exposure from agent income shocks

MoPhones Kenya · CX Automation & Self-Service Systems

Grace Period Automation —
From 2,000 Manual Requests to Self-Service

How I mapped a high-volume, manually handled support workflow, identified the root causes of repeat requests, and designed an automated self-service system that resolved the majority of cases without human intervention — reducing CX workload by 43%.

Company
MoPhones Kenya
My Role
Product Associate / PM
Domain
CX · Automation · Self-Service
Scale
2,000+ Requests/Month
Situation

A 2,000-request-per-month support bottleneck with no self-service path

MoPhones customers on device credit loans could request a grace period when facing payment difficulties — a temporary extension before the device would lock. With over 2,000 such requests arriving monthly, the entire process ran through the CX team manually. Agents logged requests, verified eligibility, updated systems, and responded to customers individually.

The process was slow, inconsistent, and unscalable. Wait times were unpredictable. Agents spent significant time on routine eligibility checks. And customers — already under financial stress — were experiencing friction at exactly the wrong moment in the relationship.

Task

Design a system that resolves most cases without human intervention

  • Reduce CX manual workload while maintaining appropriate risk controls on who qualifies
  • Improve customer experience — faster resolution, transparent process, no anxiety from waiting
  • Define eligibility logic that was fair, rules-based, and could be applied automatically without officer review
  • Instrument the system so we could measure outcomes, track abuse, and iterate on rules over time
Action

Built the eligibility logic, self-service flow, and automation infrastructure

  • Audited 3 months of grace period requests — categorised by reason, repayment history, frequency, and outcome — to understand who was requesting, why, and what the default correlation looked like
  • Defined eligibility criteria: Repayment history threshold, maximum number of prior grace periods, time since last request, and loan tenure — translating risk policy into product-executable rules
  • Designed the self-service USSD and in-app flow — customers could request a grace period, see their eligibility status, and receive confirmation in real time without contacting support
  • Coordinated with engineering on NuovoPay integration to automate device lock/unlock state changes based on grace period approval — removing the manual CX step entirely for qualifying cases
  • Built the measurement framework — tracking auto-approve rate, manual escalation rate, grace period default correlation, and customer satisfaction with the new flow
Result

43% reduction in CX manual workload — at scale

  • 43% of monthly grace period requests now resolved automatically through self-service — no CX agent required
  • Customer resolution time dropped from multi-day to near-instant for eligible requests
  • Escalation queue reduced significantly — CX team capacity freed for complex, edge-case handling that genuinely required human judgement
  • No increase in grace period default rate — the eligibility rules maintained risk quality while improving experience
Reflection

What this project is really about

The grace period system isn't just a CX efficiency project. It's a risk management product — because the decision about who gets a grace period, how many times, and under what conditions directly affects the default rate and the device recovery economics.

The key design tension was: make it easy enough that stressed customers can access it at 11pm without calling support, but structured enough that it doesn't become an exploit. Getting that balance right required understanding the customer behaviour data first — and the audit of 3 months of prior requests made that possible.

Broader Outcomes

What the automation enabled

  • A scalable support infrastructure — volume can grow without proportionally growing the CX team
  • Consistent, policy-aligned decisions — no more variation based on which agent handled the request
  • A customer experience that builds trust at a moment of financial stress — faster, clearer, and less reliant on human availability
  • A template for automating other high-volume, rules-based CX workflows across the product
